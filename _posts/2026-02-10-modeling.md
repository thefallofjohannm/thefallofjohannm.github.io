## 1. Task

In a membrane reactor, a reactive species diffuses through a semi-permeable membrane where a first-order reaction occurs. Concentration changes along the membrane thickness, and permeability may depend on temperature. Develop a 1D coupled mass diffusion-reaction model using the Finite Volume Method (FVM) that predicts concentration decay across the membrane. Evaluate the effect of reaction rate constants and membrane thickness on conversion.

## 2. Problem Description

I decided to model the removal of lactose from milk using a membrane loaded with an enzyme that breaks it down to galactose and glucose. Milk is ultra-filtered with a porous membrane made out of polyethersulfone (PES), and the enzyme used is a lactase ($\beta$-galactosidase) from *Aspergillus Oryzae*. Therefore, the concentration of lactose $c(z,t)$ will be modeled across the membrane.

## 3. Calculations

### Assumptions
* **Isothermal process:** Enzymes may degrade as temperature increases, so temperature is kept constant.
* **One-dimensional:** Only diffusion and first-order reaction in one dimension are assumed.
* **Species focus:** Only the concentration of lactose in the membrane is modeled.

### Governing Equations
The partial differential equation for this system is:

$$\frac{\partial c(z,t)}{\partial t} = D \frac{\partial^2 c(z,t)}{\partial z^2} - k\, c(z,t)$$

According to literature, milk contains about 49 g/L of lactose. The diffusivity of lactose is approximated by its value in water: $D = 0.541 \times 10^{-9}$ m²/s at 25 °C. 

The enzymatic reaction rate is described by the **Michaelis-Menten law**:

$$v = \frac{V_{\max}[S]}{K_M + [S]}$$

Where:
- **$$V_{\max}$$**: Maximum reaction velocity.
- **$$[S]$$**: Substrate concentration.
- **$$K_M$$**: Michaelis constant (concentration at half $$V_{\max}$$).

If the substrate concentration is significantly lower than $$K_M$$, it can be approximated with first-order kinetics:

$$k = \frac{V_{\max}}{K_M} c_{\text{enzyme}}$$

### Parameters and Constraints
* **Constants:** $$K_M = 0.840$$ mM, $$V_{\max} = 0.0838$$ mM/min/mg-enzyme.
* **Effective Rate:** Although bulk concentration is high, diffusion limitations inside the pores lead to lower local substrate concentrations; thus, the first-order kinetic model represents an effective reaction rate.
* **Loading:** Enzyme loading is approximately 0.2 mg-enzyme/cm³-polymer. The rate was estimated to be 100 times lower due to substrate inaccessibility in porous media.
* **Geometry:** Membrane thickness ($$L$$) is roughly 150 μm.

### Boundary and Initial Conditions
$$c(0,t) = c_{A0}$$
$$c(z,0) = 0$$

For the outlet ($z=L$), a zero-gradient (**Neumann**) boundary condition is applied. This assumes the outlet concentration is determined by the internal diffusion-reaction processes rather than an ideal sink.

---

## 4. Results and Discussion

The system was examined for three different membrane thicknesses (**100, 150, 200 μm**) and three different reaction rate constants (**½k, k, 2k**).

### Key Findings
* **Rate Effect:** Doubling the rate constant while keeping length constant significantly increases conversion (e.g., at L = 100 μm, conversion jumps from **32 %** to **51 %**).
* **Length Effect:** Increasing the membrane length also improves conversion. 
* **Equivalency:** Interestingly, for this specific setup, doubling the reaction rate constant yields a similar conversion to increasing the membrane thickness by **50 μm**.

**Table 1: Conversion achieved in each setup**

| Thickness | ½k | k | 2k |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **100 μm** | 32% | 51% | 70% |
| **150 μm** | 54% | 73% | 88% |
| **200 μm** | 71% | 86% | 95% |

---

## 5. Conclusion

The removal of lactose from milk was successfully modeled. We found that in this specific configuration, the reaction rate constant and membrane thickness have a roughly interchangeable impact on performance: a 2x increase in $$k$$ is approximately equivalent to a **50 μm increase in L**.

### Function

```matlab
function [dydt] = model_project(t,y,pars)

% Model of coupled diffusion-reaction. Lactose penetrates through porous PES
% membrane where an enzyme lactase is embedded. Lactose is being broken
% down to glucose and galactose. The concentration of lactose is modeled.


% Unpack parameters
D25 = pars(1);    % m2/s
cE = pars(2);      % kg/m3
VMAX = pars(3);    % mM/s/kg
KM = pars(4);        % mM
L_membr = pars(5);  % m
cA0 = pars(6);      % mol/m3
k = pars(7);   % volumetric reaction rate constant, 1/s/m3



%Unpack state variables
N = length(y);
c = y;



%% 1) Discretization & geometrie
% Discretization
dL = L_membr/(N-0.5);
L = zeros(N+1,1);
L(1) = 0;
for i = 2:N+1
    L(i) = (i-1.5)*dL;
end

% Surface areas between FVs
A = zeros(N+1,1);
for i=1:N+1
    A(i) = 0.5; %m2 (area of membrane taken from literature)
end

% Volumes of FVs
V = zeros(N,1);
for i = 1:N
    V(i) = A(i)*(L(i+1)-L(i));
end


%% 2) Fluxes across FV boundaries
j = zeros(N+1,1);
% Start (left side)
j(1) = -D25*(c(1)-cA0)/(dL/2);
% the middle
for i=2:N
    j(i) = -D25*(c(i)-c(i-1))/dL;
end
% End (right side)
j(N+1) = -D25*(c(N-1) - c(N))/(dL/2); 

%% 3) Balance of Finite volumes
dcdt = zeros(N,1);
for i=1:N
    dcdt(i) = j(i)*A(i) - j(i+1)*A(i+1) -k*c(i)*V(i);
    dcdt(i) = dcdt(i)/V(i);
end

% Send a column vector
dydt = dcdt;
```
